# MEET THE CHARACTERS OF FUN-GUS
## Video Script (~3.5 minutes)

> **Format:** Narrator-driven with character dialogue. Animated character reveals with SILLIUM network visuals. Educational mycology callouts appear as glowing "network data packets" on screen.
>
> **Tone:** Warm, inviting, wonder-filled. Think nature documentary meets Pixar behind-the-scenes.
>
> **Music:** Organic ambient score — soft pulses synced to network glow. Builds in warmth with each new character.

---

### COLD OPEN (0:00–0:15)

**[VISUAL: Black screen. A single cyan thread of light pulses across the darkness. Then another. Then dozens. A vast underground network illuminates — SILLIUM awakening.]**

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Beneath your feet, right now, there's a network older than the internet. Older than language. Older than almost anything alive.

**[The threads converge on a warm white glow.]**

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
And it has a guide.

---

### FUN-GUS (0:15–0:35)

**[Fun-Gus steps into the light. Warm white glow radiates downward toward the soil. He looks directly at camera with a gentle, curious smile.]**

**FUN-GUS:**
Welcome to SILLIUM. I'm Fun-Gus. And this... is the wood wide web.

**[Camera pulls back to reveal the vast mycelium network stretching in every direction, threads pulsing with cyan light.]**

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Fun-Gus is your guide to the world's oldest living network — a mycelium web that connects trees, shares nutrients, and sends warnings across entire forests.

> **MYCOLOGY LESSON — Data Packet #1:**
> *Real mycelium networks can span thousands of acres. A single fungal organism in Oregon's Blue Mountains covers 2,385 acres — making it the largest living thing on Earth.*

**FUN-GUS:**
Every thread you see? That's a conversation. Ready to meet the family?

---

### SPORA (0:35–0:55)

**[A tiny amber spore drifts into frame, glowing unstably — flickering bright, then dim, then bright again. She bumps into a network thread and giggles.]**

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
This is Spora. A newborn spore still learning how the network works.

**SPORA:**
*(touching a glowing thread, eyes wide)*
Wait — I can feel the whole forest through this?

**FUN-GUS:**
*(warm laugh)*
Not yet. But you will.

> **MYCOLOGY LESSON — Data Packet #2:**
> *A single mushroom can release 30,000 spores per second. Each one is a potential new life — but only a tiny fraction find the right conditions to grow. Spora is one of the lucky ones.*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Spora's journey — from floating observer to full network participant — is the heart of our story.

---

### TRUFF-LOW (0:55–1:10)

**[The camera descends deep underground. Everything goes quiet. Dark. Then a slow, ancient, deep-green glow awakens.]**

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
And deep below... the memory keeper.

**[Truff-Low emerges — ancient, massive, deliberate. When he speaks, the entire network dims to listen.]**

**TRUFF-LOW:**
*(slow, resonant)*
I remember when this forest was a seed.

> **MYCOLOGY LESSON — Data Packet #3:**
> *Truffles form underground and can live for decades. They store nutrients and genetic information in dense fruiting bodies — nature's hard drives. Some truffle species haven't changed in over 100 million years.*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
When Truff-Low speaks, it matters. Because he only speaks when it does.

---

### LUMOS (1:10–1:25)

**[A nervous blue-green glow appears at the network's dark edge. It flickers brighter — then BLAZES as something startles him.]**

**LUMOS:**
*(embarrassed, dimming himself)*
Sorry. Sorry. I do that when I'm... everything, actually.

> **MYCOLOGY LESSON — Data Packet #4:**
> *Mycena chlorophos is a real bioluminescent mushroom. It glows blue-green in the dark — scientists believe the light attracts insects that help spread spores. Lumos can't turn off because that glow is how his species survives.*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Lumos is the network's lamplighter — venturing into the dark tunnels everyone else avoids. He doesn't think he's brave. He just can't stand anyone being stuck in the dark.

---

### CHANTY (1:25–1:40)

**[Golden particles drift through the air as a regal, trumpet-shaped figure steps into an underground clearing. Other characters gather like an audience.]**

**CHANTY:**
*(theatrical, a golden glow pulsing from her ridges)*
Gather round. I have a story about how this forest survived the Great Drought. It begins with a single thread of generosity...

> **MYCOLOGY LESSON — Data Packet #5:**
> *Golden chanterelles form symbiotic partnerships with tree roots called mycorrhizae — "fungus roots." The tree gives the chanterelle sugar from photosynthesis. The chanterelle gives the tree water and minerals from the soil. Neither can thrive alone.*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Chanty carries the network's stories. Because data fades. But stories? Stories last.

---

### PUFF (1:40–1:55)

**[A perfectly round white ball bounces into frame. Eyes WIDE. Already cracking with golden spore dust.]**

**PUFF:**
GUYS! GUYS! I HAVE NEWS! I—

**[He explodes in a massive cloud of golden spores. Every character in frame is covered.]**

**BRACKET:**
*(wiping spore dust off, deadpan)*
Could you just... NOT... for five minutes?

> **MYCOLOGY LESSON — Data Packet #6:**
> *Giant puffballs can grow to the size of a soccer ball and release up to 7 TRILLION spores in a single burst. If every spore from one puffball grew, the resulting fungal mass would be 800 times the size of Earth.*

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Puff can't contain his excitement. Literally. But when the network needs an emergency broadcast sent to everyone at once? That "flaw" becomes the most powerful signal in SILLIUM.

---

### INKY & MOREL (1:55–2:15)

**[Split screen. Left: Inky in her quiet archive, cap dissolving into beautiful black-purple ink as she writes. Right: Morel standing alone at the edge of scorched earth, tiny green shoots emerging at his feet.]**

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Inky is the scribe. Every record she creates costs her a piece of herself — her cap dissolves into ink. She chose this. Because truth deserves to be permanent.

**INKY:**
*(carefully recording, ink trailing from her fingertips)*
Everything worth creating costs something. That's what makes it worth creating.

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
And Morel? He appears where fire has been. Nobody knows where he goes between disasters. Nobody knows how old he is.

**MOREL:**
*(staring at new growth, quiet)*
The soil asked me to come. So I came.

> **MYCOLOGY LESSON — Data Packet #7:**
> *Ink cap mushrooms really do dissolve themselves — a process called autodigestion or deliquescence. The black liquid disperses their spores. And morel mushrooms genuinely appear after forest fires, among the first organisms to colonize scorched earth. Destruction and renewal are written into their biology.*

---

### SNAPPER, STENCH & PHYSA (2:15–2:40)

**[Quick montage: Three characters in their worlds.]**

**[Snapper — sleek blue-gray, positioned protectively near Mother Oak's roots. Silk-like trap threads extend into the soil behind her.]**

**SNAPPER:**
*(calm, watchful)*
I don't have enemies. Just... nutrients I haven't processed yet.

> **MYCOLOGY LESSON — Data Packet #8:**
> *Oyster mushrooms are predatory. They produce toxins that paralyze nematode worms, then consume them for nitrogen. Your grocery store mushrooms are hunters.*

**[Stench — standing alone, insect friends buzzing around his lattice structure. Warm but isolated.]**

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Stench does essential work — his scent draws the insects that carry spores to places the network can't reach. But it means everyone keeps their distance.

**STENCH:**
*(dry, practiced)*
Oh, you're leaving? Can't imagine why.

> **MYCOLOGY LESSON — Data Packet #9:**
> *Stinkhorn fungi produce a powerful odor that smells like rotting meat — not as a flaw, but as a strategy. Flies land on the stinkhorn, get coated in spores, and carry them to new locations. The "worst" smell in the forest does one of the most important jobs.*

**[Physa — bright yellow, tendrils extending in every direction simultaneously, mapping a complex junction. Network patterns compute visibly inside her translucent body.]**

**PHYSA:**
*(focused, matter-of-fact)*
I am NOT a fungus. I'm a protist. There IS a difference.

> **MYCOLOGY LESSON — Data Packet #10:**
> *Slime molds were classified as fungi for centuries before scientists realized they're something else entirely. And they're geniuses — Physarum polycephalum can solve mazes, replicate Tokyo's rail map, and find the most efficient path between food sources. No brain required.*

---

### RUSSULA, BIRDIE & TARDI (2:40–3:00)

**[Russula poses in perfect light, vivid scarlet cap gleaming. She shifts slightly to hide a hairline crack.]**

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
Russula is the brightest thing in any room. And the most fragile.

> **MYCOLOGY LESSON — Data Packet #11:**
> *Russula mushrooms are famous for two things: vivid colors ranging from scarlet to purple to green — and extreme brittleness. Their stems snap like chalk. Beauty and fragility in the same organism.*

**[Birdie — cup-shaped, spectacles on, clipboard tucked under arm, launching a spore packet with pinpoint accuracy across a network junction.]**

**BIRDIE:**
*(adjusting spectacles)*
Target acquired. Three zones. Wind correction applied. And honey, have you eaten today?

> **MYCOLOGY LESSON — Data Packet #12:**
> *Bird's nest fungi really do look like tiny bird nests with eggs. The "eggs" are spore packets called peridioles. When a raindrop hits the cup at the right angle, it launches the packet up to a meter away — a precision catapult system evolved over millions of years.*

**[Tardi — tiny, adorable, eight-legged, standing at the edge of a toxic zone looking mildly excited.]**

**TARDI:**
*(genuine confusion)*
What's the worst that could happen?

> **MYCOLOGY LESSON — Data Packet #13:**
> *Tardigrades aren't fungi — they're micro-animals. But they live in the same soil world. They can survive boiling water, absolute zero, the vacuum of space, and radiation levels that would kill a human 1,000 times over. They're the toughest animals on Earth.*

---

### RHIZA & THE NETWORK (3:00–3:20)

**[The camera finds the place where fungal threads meet tree roots. A luminous figure shimmers between two forms — half white hyphae, half brown rootlets. Nutrient particles flow through her like golden fireflies.]**

**NARRATOR (V.O.):**
And then there's Rhiza. She's not a mushroom. She's not a root. She's the handshake between them.

**RHIZA:**
*(serene, glowing at her center)*
They give sugar. We give minerals. I make sure the exchange is fair. Without the handshake... there is no network.

> **MYCOLOGY LESSON — Data Packet #14:**
> *The mycorrhizal interface — where fungi physically connect to tree roots — is what makes the entire "wood wide web" work. Over 90% of land plants depend on these fungal partnerships. The forest doesn't just have a network. The forest IS a network.*

---

### CLOSE (3:20–3:35)

**[Wide shot: all the characters together in SILLIUM's central hub. Threads pulse with light. Fun-Gus stands at the center. Spora floats nearby, her glow a little steadier now.]**

**FUN-GUS:**
*(looking at camera)*
This is just the beginning. We haven't even told you about the bacteria, the insects, or the villains who want to tear this network apart.

**SPORA:**
*(small, excited)*
Or the part where I save the—

**FUN-GUS:**
*(gentle hand on her shoulder)*
No spoilers.

**[The network pulses once — warm, alive, connected. Fade to black.]**

The world beneath your feet is more alive than you ever imagined. And it has stories to tell.
