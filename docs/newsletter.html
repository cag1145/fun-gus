Feature Story

"The Day the Oak Called for Help"

By Chanty, Network Storyteller

It started with a whisper. Not the kind you hear with ears — the kind you feel through the threads. A faint, golden pulse traveling through the deepest parts of the Sillium. Something was wrong.

I was telling a story to a group of young sporelings when the signal hit. My story-dust flickered. The threads beneath me hummed with urgency.

The message came from the East Ridge: Old Grandmother Oak was under attack.

Grandmother Oak is no ordinary tree. She's been standing for over 300 years. She feeds seedlings. She sends warnings. She is the heart of this part of the network.

And caterpillars — hundreds of them — were eating her leaves.

Here's the thing most surface-dwellers don't know: when a tree is attacked, it doesn't just sit there. Grandmother Oak sent a chemical distress signal through the network.

The signal raced through the threads. Within minutes, every tree within a quarter-mile had received the warning. The maples made their leaves taste bitter. The beeches thickened their leaf coatings. Even the young aspens started producing defensive chemicals — all before a single caterpillar reached them.

Meanwhile, Stench called in his insect allies. A squadron of parasitic wasps arrived within the hour.

By sunset, the attack was contained. And those seedlings she's been feeding? They kept getting their sugar deliveries the whole time. Because that's what the network does: it protects its own.

The Real Science: Trees really do send chemical warning signals when attacked! Research by Dr. Suzanne Simard and others has shown these signals travel through mycorrhizal networks underground AND through the air. Neighboring trees begin producing defensive chemicals before the insects even arrive.

Spora's Journal

Entry #12: "I Found My Signal"

By Spora

Dear Journal,

I DID IT.

I know that probably doesn't sound like a big deal. Everyone else sends messages all the time. Puff sends about forty per hour (most of them are just "HI!" but still).

But for me, sending even ONE message has been... hard. My filaments would flicker. My signal would start to form and then — nothing.

Fun-Gus kept saying, "Don't force it." Chanty told me, "Say what you actually feel." Even Truff-Low said: "The forest doesn't just need its past. It needs its future. That's you."

Today, I went back to the same thread. I put my tendril against it. And instead of trying to send a perfect signal, I just... said what was true.

I'm here. I'm new. And I want to be part of this.

The thread lit up. A small, steady glow. Warm amber-gold. And it held. For the first time, my signal was stable.

And then I felt responses coming back. From EVERYWHERE. Little pulses of acknowledgment. Fun-Gus just looked at me and said, "Welcome to the network. For real this time."

Today, for the first time, I feel like I belong here. I feel connected.

— Spora

P.S. Puff says my signal was "THE BEST SIGNAL HE'S EVER SEEN" but I think he says that about everything.

Ask Fun-Gus

Your questions, answered

"Why do mushrooms pop up after it rains?" — Ella, age 8, Portland

Great question! Mushrooms are the "fruit" of the fungus — like apples on a tree. The real organism is the mycelium underground, there ALL the time. When it rains, the soil gets saturated and the fungus says, "NOW'S our chance!" and pushes mushrooms up to release spores — sometimes overnight!

"Can trees really talk?" — James, age 6, Minneapolis

They don't use words like we do. But they absolutely communicate! Trees send chemical signals through the fungal network — kind of like text messages through underground cables. Scientists have measured these signals in labs and forests worldwide.

"Is Sillium a real place?" — Priya, age 9, Toronto

Sillium is our name for the fungal network in the Fun-Gus stories. In real life, scientists call it a mycorrhizal network — or sometimes the Wood Wide Web. The real one exists under every forest on the planet. You can't see it without a microscope, but it's there. Right under your feet.

Activity: Build Your Own Network

Materials Needed

  • A ball of yarn or string
  • 5–10 friends, family members, or stuffed animals
  • Index cards and markers
  • A room or outdoor space

Instructions

  1. Assign roles. Each person picks a character.
  2. Create the network. One person (Fun-Gus) holds the yarn end and passes the ball to another, keeping hold of their end.
  3. Keep connecting. Each person holds their section and passes to someone new. Soon you'll have a web — just like mycelium!
  4. Test the network. One person tugs gently. Can the others feel it? That's how signals travel!
  5. Try removing a connection. What happens? The network weakens. Pick it back up — it recovers. THIS is why biodiversity matters.
