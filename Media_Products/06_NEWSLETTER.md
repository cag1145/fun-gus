## FEATURE STORY

### "THE DAY THE OAK CALLED FOR HELP"
*By Chanty, Network Storyteller*

It started with a whisper.

Not the kind you hear with ears — the kind you feel through the threads. A faint, golden pulse traveling through the deepest parts of the Sillium. Something was wrong.

I was telling a story to a group of young sporelings (it was a good one — about the time a mother birch saved three seedlings during the Great Drought of 1987) when the signal hit. My story-dust flickered. The threads beneath me hummed with urgency.

The message came from the East Ridge: **Old Grandmother Oak was under attack.**

Now, Grandmother Oak is no ordinary tree. She's been standing for over 300 years. Her roots reach deeper than any tree in this forest, and she's connected to more fungal partners than I can count. She feeds seedlings. She sends warnings. She is, in every way that matters, the heart of this part of the network.

And caterpillars — hundreds of them — were eating her leaves.

Here's the thing most surface-dwellers don't know: when a tree is attacked, it doesn't just sit there and take it. Grandmother Oak did what trees do — she sent a chemical distress signal through the network.

I felt it. Fun-Gus felt it. Rhiza, who manages the nutrient exchange at Grandmother Oak's roots, felt it first and loudest.

The signal raced through the threads. Within minutes, every tree within a quarter-mile had received the warning. And they responded.

The maples made their leaves taste bitter. The beeches thickened their leaf coatings. Even the young aspens, barely ten years old, started producing defensive chemicals — all before a single caterpillar reached them.

Meanwhile, Stench called in his insect allies. A squadron of parasitic wasps arrived within the hour — caterpillars' natural enemies, summoned by the chemical signals the trees were pumping into the air.

By sunset, the attack was contained. Grandmother Oak lost some leaves, but she'll grow them back. She always does.

And those seedlings she's been feeding? They kept getting their sugar deliveries the whole time. Not one was cut off. Because that's what the network does: it protects its own.

I recorded the whole event in story-dust. Inky wrote it on the cavern wall. And now I'm telling you.

Because this isn't fiction. This is what happens in real forests, every single day.

You just can't see it. Yet.

---

**📖 THE REAL SCIENCE:** Trees really do send chemical warning signals when attacked by insects! Research by Dr. Suzanne Simard and others has shown that these signals travel through mycorrhizal networks underground AND through the air as volatile organic compounds. Neighboring trees that receive the warning begin producing defensive chemicals before the insects even arrive. This has been documented in forests worldwide.

## SPORA'S JOURNAL

### Entry #12: "I Found My Signal"
*By Spora*

Dear Journal,

I DID IT.

I know that probably doesn't sound like a big deal. Everyone else sends messages all the time. Puff sends about forty per hour (most of them are just "HI!" but still). Chanty sends stories that flow through the whole network like golden rivers.

But for me, sending even ONE message has been... hard.

I've been trying for days. My filaments would flicker. My signal would start to form and then — nothing. Like trying to speak and having your voice disappear.

Fun-Gus kept saying, "Don't force it." Chanty told me, "Say what you actually feel, not what you think you should say." Even Truff-Low, who barely talks at all, said something I keep thinking about: "The forest doesn't just need its past. It needs its future. That's you."

No pressure or anything.

Today, I went back to the same thread I've been practicing on. I put my tendril against it. I closed my eyes. And instead of trying to send a perfect signal, I just... said what was true.

*I'm here. I'm new. And I want to be part of this.*

The thread lit up.

Not with some massive, network-shaking pulse. Just a small, steady glow. Warm amber-gold. And it held. It didn't flicker. It didn't sputter.

For the first time, my signal was stable.

And then — and this is the part I still can't believe — I felt responses coming back. From EVERYWHERE. Little pulses of acknowledgment. Other fungi, other nodes, other parts of the forest I've never even been to. They heard me. They answered.

Puff burst, obviously. Lumos glowed so bright I could see him from three junctions away. And Fun-Gus just looked at me with this smile and said, "Welcome to the network. For real this time."

I don't know what tomorrow will bring. I don't know if my signal will hold every time. But today, for the first time, I feel like I belong here.

I feel connected.

— Spora

*P.S. Puff says I should add that my signal was "THE BEST SIGNAL HE'S EVER SEEN" but I think he says that about everything.*

## ASK FUN-GUS

### Your Questions, Answered

**Q: "Why do mushrooms pop up after it rains?" — Ella, age 8, Portland**

Great question, Ella! Here's the short answer: mushrooms are the "fruit" of the fungus — like apples on an apple tree. The real organism is the mycelium underground, which is there ALL the time.

But mushrooms need a lot of water to grow quickly. When it rains, the soil gets saturated, and the fungus says, "NOW'S our chance!" It pushes mushrooms up through the soil to release spores — sometimes overnight!

That's why you see them "appear" so fast. They're not growing from nothing — the mycelium was already there, waiting for the right conditions. The mushroom is just the grand finale.

**Q: "Can trees really talk?" — James, age 6, Minneapolis**

They don't use words like we do, James. But they absolutely communicate!

Trees send chemical signals through the fungal network — kind of like text messages through underground cables. These signals carry information about danger (insects, disease), requests for help, and even care packages (sugar sent from parent trees to baby trees).

Scientists have measured these signals in labs and forests around the world. So yes — trees talk. They just speak chemistry, not English.

**Q: "Is Sillium a real place?" — Priya, age 9, Toronto**

Sillium is our name for the fungal network in the Fun-Gus stories. In real life, scientists call it a **mycorrhizal network** — or sometimes the **Wood Wide Web** (isn't that a great name?).

The real Wood Wide Web exists under every forest on the planet. It's not a single cavern like in our stories — it's trillions of microscopic threads woven through the soil, connecting tree roots to fungal partners. You can't see it without a microscope, but it's there.

So Sillium isn't a "real place" you can visit — but the network it's based on? That's 100% real. And it's right under your feet.

## ACTIVITY: BUILD YOUR OWN NETWORK

### Materials Needed:
- A ball of yarn or string (any color — but cyan or gold would be very Sillium)
- 5–10 friends, family members, or stuffed animals
- Index cards and markers
- A room or outdoor space

### Instructions:

1. **Assign roles.** Each person picks a character card from the Fun-Gus trading card set (or make your own). Write the character name on an index card and hold it.

2. **Create the network.** One person (Fun-Gus) holds the end of the yarn. They pass the ball to another person (say, Rhiza), keeping hold of their end. Now Rhiza holds the yarn and passes the ball to someone else.

3. **Keep connecting.** Each person holds their section of yarn and passes the ball to someone new. Soon you'll have a web of string connecting everyone — just like the mycelium network connects trees!

4. **Test the network.** One person (a "tree") tugs gently on their string. Can the others feel it? That's how chemical signals travel through the real network.

5. **Try removing a connection.** What happens when someone drops their string? The network weakens. What happens when they pick it back up? It recovers. THIS is why biodiversity matters — the more connections, the stronger the network.

**Discussion:** What happens if you remove Fun-Gus from the center? What if Spora joins for the first time? How does each new connection change the network?
