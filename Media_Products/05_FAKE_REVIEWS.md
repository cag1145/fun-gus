### ★★★★½ — THE NEW YORK TIMES

**"Underground, a Quiet Revolution in Children's Television"**

*By Margaret Chen | Television Critic | March 15, 2027*

In an era of hyper-saturated children's programming, where every new show competes for eyeballs with louder colors and faster cuts, "Fun-Gus: The Forest Has WiFi" does something radical: it slows down. It looks at the dirt. And it finds, beneath that dirt, something extraordinary.

The premise sounds deceptively simple — two kids follow their teacher into a forest, step into a fairy ring, shrink to spore-size, and discover an underground world of talking fungi. But what creators have built here is no ordinary cartoon. This is mycology as myth, ecology as epic, and science education as genuinely compelling storytelling.

The cast of fungal characters — over a dozen, each based on a real species — is immediately lovable without being saccharine. Fun-Gus himself, voiced with tremendous warmth, is the mentor figure every child deserves: patient, funny, and incapable of condescension. But it's Spora, the newborn spore who "doesn't know how to do anything yet," who becomes the show's emotional anchor. Her journey from uncertainty to belonging is handled with a delicacy that would make Pixar proud.

The science is meticulous. The creative team clearly studied mycorrhizal networks, bioluminescence, and fungal reproduction in depth — but the show wears its research lightly. Kids will learn that trees talk to each other underground without ever feeling lectured. Adults will learn things they didn't know.

And then there's Ms. Hawthorn.

The children's teacher is, on the surface, exactly what she appears: a warm, encouraging guide who happens to know a lot about forests. But the show layers in hints — a robin that never leaves her shoulder, flowers that lean toward her, an agelessness in her eyes — that suggest something deeper. Something folkloric. It's a masterclass in dual-audience storytelling: children see a kind teacher, adults see a mystery they can't quite name.

Production values are stunning. The underground Sillium sequences glow with a bioluminescent beauty that rivals the best animated films. The color language — cyan for communication, amber for growth, purple for ancient memory — creates an intuitive visual grammar that even pre-readers can follow.

If there's a criticism, it's that the first episode tries to introduce too many characters. The rapid-fire meetings in the middle section (Lumos, Puff, Chanty, Rhiza, and more in quick succession) feel rushed compared to the show's otherwise patient pace. Future episodes that focus on individual characters will likely solve this.

But this is a minor quibble about a major achievement. "Fun-Gus" arrives at exactly the right moment — when children need stories about connection, cooperation, and the staggering intelligence of the natural world. That it teaches real science while doing so is a bonus. That it made this critic tear up during a scene involving a nervous spore sending her first message is something else entirely.

**Rating: ★★★★½ out of ★★★★★**

*"Fun-Gus: The Forest Has WiFi" premieres March 22 on PBS Kids. New episodes weekly.*

### 9.2/10 — IGN FAMILY

**"Fun-Gus Grows on You — and Doesn't Let Go"**

*By David Okafor | March 18, 2027*

Look, I'll be honest: when I read the pitch — "kids shrink into an underground fungal network and meet talking mushrooms" — I expected something weird and niche. What I got was one of the most emotionally resonant pilots I've seen in children's television since the first season of "Bluey."

The animation is GORGEOUS. The Sillium sequences look like what would happen if Pixar designed a nature documentary. Every frame underground is dripping with detail — threads of mycelium pulse with light, spore particles drift through the air, and the color design tells its own story independent of dialogue.

Fun-Gus is an instant classic character. He's funny without being silly, wise without being preachy, and genuinely warm in a way that doesn't feel manufactured. His relationship with Spora — the tiny, uncertain newborn who's afraid she'll never be good enough — is the heart of the show, and it beats strong.

But here's what surprised me most: the show trusts kids. It doesn't dumb down the science. It uses words like "mycorrhizae" and "symbiosis" and "hyphae" — and it defines them through story, not flashcards. My 7-year-old watched the pilot and then spent 20 minutes telling me about how trees share food underground. She'd learned more in one episode than in a semester of science class.

The character design deserves special mention. Each fungal character is based on a real species, and their personalities grow organically from real biology: Puff the puffball explodes with excitement (because puffballs really burst to spread spores), Lumos the bioluminescent mushroom gets brighter when scared (because real bioluminescent fungi glow in the dark), Inky the ink cap dissolves her own cap to write (because ink caps really autodigest). It's biology-as-character-design, and it's brilliant.

The one area I'd flag for parents: the Snapper sequence, where a parasitic nematode threatens the network, is mildly intense for very young viewers (ages 3-4). It's handled gently — Snapper neutralizes the threat calmly and quickly — but sensitive kids may need a heads-up.

**The bottom line:** "Fun-Gus" is that rare kids' show that adults will genuinely want to watch alongside their children. It's beautiful, it's smart, it's emotional, and it might just be the most important new show on children's television.

**Score: 9.2/10 — AMAZING**

### NATIONAL SCIENCE TEACHERS ASSOCIATION — RECOMMENDED

**"Fun-Gus: A Model for Science Communication in Children's Media"**

*Reviewed by Dr. Angela Reyes, Biology Education Specialist | NSTA Journal, May 2027*

In evaluating "Fun-Gus: The Forest Has WiFi" for classroom suitability, our panel was struck by the show's unusual commitment to scientific accuracy within an engaging narrative framework.

**Scientific Accuracy: A**
The show's representation of mycorrhizal networks, fungal reproduction, bioluminescence, and interspecies symbiosis is consistent with current peer-reviewed research. Specific citations to the work of Dr. Suzanne Simard (mother trees), Merlin Sheldrake (fungal intelligence), and published studies on chemical signaling in forest ecosystems are evident in the show's scientific foundations. No significant factual errors were identified.

**Pedagogical Value: A**
The show employs a "story-first, science-embedded" model that aligns with current best practices in informal science education. Key vocabulary (mycelium, hyphae, symbiosis, mycorrhizae, spore) is introduced contextually through character interaction rather than didactic exposition. The "Real Science" segment following each episode provides explicit connections between fiction and fact.

**Character-Science Integration: A+**
Most remarkable is the show's approach to character design. Each character's personality, abilities, and story arc grow directly from real fungal biology. This is not cosmetic — the science IS the character. Puff's excitability comes from puffball spore dispersal. Lumos's anxiety-brightness from bioluminescent defense. Inky's sacrifice from ink cap autodigestion. This approach makes the science memorable because it's inseparable from characters children love.

**Classroom Recommendation:**
Suitable for grades K-5 science curriculum. Episodes align with NGSS standards for ecosystems, interdependent relationships, and structure/function in living organisms. Supplementary materials (discussion questions, hands-on activities) are available at fungusbook.com.

**Rating: Highly Recommended**
